Before they go public: these are the real ways a Canadian with a small account can get in before or at the IPO price — ranked by how realistic they are for you today.
WORKS WITH ~$100

FrontFundr — Canadian equity crowdfunding

Buy shares in private Canadian startups from as little as $100–$250, before any IPO. This is true pre-public investing, legal for regular (non-accredited) investors.

The catch: most startups never IPO. Your money can be locked up for years with no way to sell. Treat any single deal as money you can afford to lose entirely.

WORKS WITH A SMALL ACCOUNT

Questrade IPO Centre — new issues at the issue price

Questrade lets you request shares in Canadian IPOs and new issues at the issue price — the price institutions pay, before the stock ever trades. No commission, and no big minimum beyond the cost of the shares.

The catch: allocation isn't guaranteed (hot deals get oversubscribed), and it's mostly Canadian listings, not US ones.

NOT YET — NEEDS $10K+ & ACCREDITATION

EquityZen / Forge — late-stage pre-IPO shares

These marketplaces sell shares of big private names (think SpaceX-tier companies) before they list. This is what people usually mean by "pre-IPO investing."

The catch: you must be an accredited investor and minimums start around US$5,000–10,000. A goal to work toward, not a starting point.

About those "$2 to $200 in a day" IPOs: those are tiny low-float listings (like HKD in 2022, which spiked +32,000% then fell ~99%). The spike happens because almost no shares trade — and by the time a normal person can buy, the crash is usually next. The calendar tab flags low-float IPOs so you can see them coming, but understand: people who buy the spike are the exit for people who caused it.

How the score works

Every IPO gets 0–100 based on: float size (fewer shares = bigger potential moves, but bigger crashes), deal size (small deals move more), share price (low-priced IPOs attract momentum traders), exchange (NASDAQ/NYSE get the most attention), and status (a "priced" deal is confirmed; "expected" can slip or cancel).

70+ = Hot (high volatility potential, high risk) · 50–69 = Watch · under 50 = Pass.

A high score means "likely to move a lot," not "likely to go up." Volatility cuts both ways.

Why you can't buy the IPO price at your broker

The "IPO price" you see in headlines goes to institutions the night before. When trading opens for everyone else, the price has often already jumped — that jump is called the pop, and buying after the pop is where most retail losses happen. The Pre-IPO tab shows the ways around this.

The lockup — the date that matters most

Insiders and early investors are banned from selling for ~180 days after the IPO. When that lockup expires, a flood of shares often hits the market and the price dips. If you like a company long-term, the post-lockup dip is frequently a better entry than IPO day.

Rules that keep a $100 account alive

1. Never put everything in one deal — one bad IPO shouldn't end you.
2. If a low-float IPO spikes and you're somehow in it — sell into strength. They almost never hold the high.
3. If you missed the spike, you missed it. Chasing is how small accounts die.
4. Paper-trade your first 5 picks (write down entry/exit, don't buy) and check your hit rate before risking real money.
